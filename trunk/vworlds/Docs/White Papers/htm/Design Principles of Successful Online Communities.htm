Abstract

This study is directed toward Microsofts project to create online graphical virtual communities. In this report we provide a general discussion of the challenges that will have to be met in the online community and suggest a set of design principles to guide the construction of the community. These design principles derive from a large body of research which has attempted to identify the distinguishing characteristics of successful, self-regulating communities. We enumerate the key challenges and offer design recommendations.

Introduction

We report here on a set of effective means of cultivating social solidarity and community in online interactive environments. These design principles derive from a large body of research which has attempted to identify the distinguishing characteristics of successful, self-regulating communities. Online communities share a great deal with communities built around more traditional face-to-face communication, but we highlight places where online environments offer special opportunities for or challenges to the formation of robust social relationships.

Promoting Community

Why is community important?

It is worth asking at the start what communities are and why they should be a guiding consideration for the development of a computer network application. The term community is ambiguous. It is used to describe groups that range from neighbors to nations and levels of solidarity from the personal to the professional. Community has recently become a fashionable term. Its popularity symbolizes a growing sense of social isolation and a growing concern for safety and civility. But community is neither a simple nor uniformly positive thing. Nostalgia may tinge our memories, but community has always been a conflictual and frustrating process. But as face-to-face community has frayed, computer networks have increasingly looked like a new resource for its revitalization. The spontaneous formation and robust development of thousands of online groups and self-proclaimed communities is a unique and powerful phenomenon.

What defines a community?

A community is more than a group of co-present people. Generically, a community can be understood as a group of people connected by ongoing social relations and bound together by a common interest or shared circumstance. For us, the key feature that sets communities apart from other forms of social organizations is the presence of a commons. A commons is a form of social organization that allows open access to a collectively produced or managed resource (see The Possibility of Cooperation, later in this introduction). We define a successful community as a self-sustaining collective organization that produces resources for its own maintenance and for other groups use.

Why should community guide the design of the network?

Community is essential to the development of commercial online network systems because it is the only way to convert commodified cyberspace into specialized cyberplace. To do this, one must create something that cannot be easily reproduced and transferred elsewhere. The roots of community bind it to its place in the same way that a vine is bound to a trellis.

Community does more than merely anchor a group of people to a certain system. Communities are the best form of social organization to encourage members to create their own content. Therefore, commerce should be grown out of community, rather than trying to establish a community within a virtual shopping mall. Communities create a form of content that goes beyond the standard offerings of online services.

What are the challenges to the creation of community?

Although they are useful and robust, the formation and maintenance of a community faces significant challenges. Our perspective is that the tensions inherent in all community cannot be resolved exclusively through technical means. Powerful tools must be connected and coordinated within a social framework. Social systems must be dealt with as organisms that must be cultivated and grown, not designed and built. The key challenge is to cultivate cooperation by balancing the tension between individual and collective interests.

The Possibility of Cooperation

Individual versus Collective Rationality

At the root of the problem of cooperation is the fact that there is often a tension between individual and collective rationality (Kollock and Smith 1995). This is to say that in many situations, behavior that is reasonable and justifiable for the individual leads to a poorer outcome for all. Such situations are termed social dilemmas and underlie many of the most serious social problems we face. One of the most famous models of social dilemmas is the tragedy of the commons (Hardin 1968). Hardin described a group of herders having open access to a common parcel of land on which they could let their cows graze. It is in each herders interest to put as many cows as possible onto the land, even if the commons is damaged as a result. The herder receives all the benefits from the additional cows and the damage to the commons is shared by the entire group. Yet if all herders make this individually reasonable decision, the commons is destroyed and all will suffer.

A related model of the tension between individual and collective rationality is the challenge of providing public goods. A public good is a resource from which all may benefit, regardless of whether they have helped create the good (for example, public television or a community improvement project). The temptation is to enjoy a public good without contributing to its production, but if all reach this decision, the good is never created and all suffer.

The tragedy of the commons and the challenge of providing public goods share a common feature: At the heart of each of these models in the free-rider problem. Whenever one person cannot be excluded from the benefits that others provide, each person is motivated not to contribute to the joint effort, but to free-ride on the efforts of others. If all participants choose to free-ride, the collective benefit will not be produced. The temptation to free-ride, however, may dominate the decision process and thus all will end up where no one wanted to be (Ostrom 1990, 6).

The tension between individual and collective interests is at the heart of the challenges that must be met if an online community is to be successful. In the next two sections, we discuss the most important social dilemmas that exist in online communities and offer a set of recommendations for solving these dilemmas.

Key Challenges to be Faced in the Community

In this section, we offer a description and analysis of the key challenges that will be faced in the online community. In particular, we examine the following tasks that must be accomplished in a successful community:

·         Encourage active contributions to the community.

·         Manage bandwidth and other common resources.

·         Coordinate activities and interests.

·         Establish norms and rules of decorum.

Encourage Active Contributions to the Community

Encouraging the users of this system to create content is the central challenge. Microsoft will not be able to create all the content needed for the world, nor will third party providers be able to fill the remaining gap. The users must be able to extend, create, and manipulate the world. This requires a well-designed set of tools that allows new members to immediately begin modifying the world, while providing advanced members with sophisticated tools for building more complicated objects.

Contributions to the community

While Microsoft may provide many of the broad outlines of the virtual world, there are many things that the users themselves must provide.These contributions take two basic forms: contributions to the objects in the world and contributions to the social fabric that holds together the groups that produce and utilize those objects.It is important to recognize that the former depends upon the latter.

Users will be relied upon to construct a range of objects, spaces, and activities.In order for the environment to be compelling, the creation of new aspects to the world must be an ongoing processthe world will never be complete.But all forms of creation in a social context are subject to the constraints of social dilemmas.Free riding, over-consumption and under-production plague all social groups.

The case for self-governance

It is tempting to rely upon a central authority to enforce and promote social order and continued contributions.However, central authorities have many limitations.Centralized governance is expensive and often inefficient.Further, some aspects of social cohesion cannot be externally provided.

It is far better, then, to encourage groups to provide their own means of regulating their members and ensuring that there is sufficient contribution to the continued maintenance and extension of the virtual world.The dilemma comes from the fact that some of these essential contributions are not attractive, and require that groups encourage members to provide these services.

Obstacles to contributions

There are two key obstacles to the provision of essential contributions to the community.First, there is the issue of motivation: Members must have incentives to contribute to the collective good.There is always the temptation to free ride on others contributions.Second, there is the issue of skill: Contributions will be minimized if they are too difficult or complex to create.The skill needed to create and modify the world must ramp up, allowing unsophisticated users to begin immediately, while allowing sophisticated users to increasingly push the envelope.

Whether a result of problems of motivation or skill, there are a number of classic ways in which groups fail.A group can be said to have failed when members do not contribute sufficiently to collective resources, overuse the resources that are available, become disorganized, or cannot regulate themselves.This can occur at any point along the life cycle of a group, so that a group can fail to take off, fail to sustain itself, or die from a crisis it cannot overcome.

In some cases, obstacles within the system can actually be a positive resource for community construction.For example, sophisticated use of the system is a valuable resource: It creates a scarce good (skillfully created objects, for example), encourages trade and exchange, and offers an example for others to follow.

Manage Bandwidth and Other Common Resources

Any community will have a number of finite common resources that need to be managed if the community is to be successful.These include technical resources (the carrying capacity of the connection, system resources, and display space) and social resources (limits on the demands that can be made on others time, attention, and knowledge).These common resources are especially vulnerable to depletion when there are sudden increases in the population of the community.

Scarcity of common resources

What are the common resources that must be managed in an online community?Despite the fact that virtual spaces are in many ways limitless, there are a number of finite resources that are open to common use. Scarcity intensifies the tension between individual rationality (which urges each person to use as much of a resource as they can) and collective rationality (which demands that resources be replenished and consumed at a sustainable rate).

In virtual spaces, there are two major categories of scarce resources: technical resources and social resources.Technical resources like bandwidth and displayspace are important considerations for this system.The former is a problem of all online forms of interaction.The latter is a special limitation of graphical virtual worlds.The carrying capacity of a graphical space is more limited than in a textual one where only spoken/acted events take up any room.This limitation impliesthat certain spaces will become crowded and require that systems be created to ensure equitable distribution.

Perhaps more fundamental than the technical limitations are the forms of social scarcity.The most scarce social resource in the virtual world is the attention of other members.Others competence, knowledge, time, interest, and validation are resources that no software package can provide and are the core attraction of any online system devoted to social interaction.Finding ways to encourage members to make these resources available while ensuring that members do not abuse, over-consume, or intentionally destroy these resources is the primary goal of social design.

To cultivate this balance, it may be necessary to limit a range of aspects of the virtual world, including but not limited to the population and its rate of growth, the amount of interaction, and the extent to which members can build new objects.

An example of an attempt to regulate some of these aspects in an online community can be found in a variety of text-based virtual worlds, including LambdaMOO and MediaMOO.In these communities, explosive population growth taxed the communitys capacity for socialization of new members and frequently swamped the bandwidth and servers response capacity, creating huge lag times for all (a classic commons dilemma).In addition, rapid and uncoordinated construction of new objects and spaces made the communities confused and overburdened.

The communities responded in a number of ways.LambdaMOO, for example, instituted a population growth policy, limiting the rate of new arrivals to allow the community to absorb and socialize new members.MediaMOO attempted to limit population growth and to ensure an appropriate background for new members by requiring that applicants demonstrate an academic interest in new media research.Both MOOs instituted panels of members who had authority to review publicly accessible new construction to ensure overall coherence and economy.

America Onlines (AOL) rapid increase in membership illustrates the problems associated with a sudden population growth.Technical resources were frequently exhausted, and social norms that were crafted among the original users were swamped and disrupted.While all communities need to grow, growth beyond technical and social capacities can seriously damage a community.

Common resources are often nested within one another.The larger commons created by an online service may have a number of smaller common resources within it, each with distinct qualities and needs.For example, AOL maintains a range of chat rooms which allow synchronous exchange of lines of text between a group of members visiting the same room.When an individual orsmall group of members fills the room with a continuous stream of text (often meaningless gibberish or obscenities) this small common resource is destroyed.No other conversation can easily co-exist in this environment.This has become a fairly common problem in AOL.Their response has been to officially prohibit this behavior and to have employees police chat rooms, warning and then banning offending users.While this approach has had some success, it requires that a significant number of AOL employees devote much of their time to policing.We suggest that a more effective response is to ensure that members are able to resolve this problem on their own or in small groups (see Allow Members to Govern Their Own Behavior and Encourage Members to Enforce Their Own Rules, later in this paper).

Coordinate Activities and Interests

Successful community relations require more than the motivation to work collectively.Individuals must be able to find other like-minded members, coordinate their activities in time and space, and stay focused on their collective tasks.

Coordination

A common assumption is that a kind of social gravity is sufficient to attract like-minded and interested people together.This is often not the case.Cooperation requires more than the motivation to work collectively.Individuals must be able to find others with whom to work collaboratively.Essentially, there are three basic issues: how do people find each other?, how do people identify other members of the same groups?, and how do people stay focused on their collective task?

Failure to resolve these issues results in a range of problems.Without sufficient coordination, activities get carried out in the wrong place. This leads those looking to participate in them to fail to be able to contribute, or results in redundancy, which often exacerbates resource consumption.When activities are not clearly coordinated, groups are frequently interrupted and distracted by members who mistake them for another group or area.As a result, the social space is filled with overlapping and conflicting activities.

These issues imply three related forms of coordination:interests, time, and space.It is necessary to facilitate the process of finding and identifying like-minded people.Further, the demands of a synchronous graphical space add the need to coordinate meetings in time, suggesting the need for regular daily and weekly schedules of activities.Resources must be available to aid groups in getting the word out about their existence, upcoming meetings, and the availability of new and existing resources.Finally, the organization of space is a crucial element, especially in a graphical system.The organization of spaces in relationship to one another may require that some form of zoning or architectural review panel be instituted to prevent a topological hodgepodge (see Create Clear Boundaries, later in this paper).

One solution is often referred to as the creation of Schelling points (Schelling 1960), prominent features of the environment that provide for implicit coordination.For example, travelers in New York City often look for each other under the clock in Grand Central Station.While Schelling points inevitably arise in any community, they can also be intentionally created or supported.Interest groups may, for example, employ iconic architecture, building objects that resemble their interests as a way to advertise their presence (for example, a building shaped like a town hall or a cat or a doughnut).

Establish Norms and Rules of Decorum

Communities need to reproduce themselves, to create and transmit their rules, norms, expectations and understandings to new members, and to reinforce them in existing members.Every community must maintain a body of knowledge, contain people who are willing to be teachers, people who are willing to learn, and people willing to enforce the norms and punish transgressions.

Socialization

All groups create a set of expectations about the behavior of members and nonmembers.However, a well-thought-out set of norms and expectations has little value if new members have no way to learn them.There must be a variety of resources to facilitate the learning of the local culture of a group.One common method is to rely on existing members to instruct newcomers.This is an effective method, but it can be easily overwhelmed if existing members find that all of their time in the group is spent teaching an endless stream of newcomers.A form of commons failure is possible in these situations in which the older members withdraw from the group (retreating to what has been referred to as the virtual suburbs), leaving the new group members to fend for themselves without the benefit of the existing groups culture and experience.

Creating a record of past debates and discussions that can be easily accessed by newcomers is an effective supplement to direct teaching.However, institutional memory may pose a special challenge in a graphical synchronous environment if no means of recording interaction is available.An example of an effective application of institutional memory can be found in the WELL, where almost all interactions are stored, sometimes for very long periods of time, allowing new members to return to old debates and discussions.This reduces the amount of time older members must spend socializing new members (or allows them to accomplish this by simply pointing them to the appropriate area).It also allows any newcomer to study the entire history of the group.Norms and rules will also vary from group to group and from one space to another.Thus, there is an additional issue of ensuring that boundaries are clearly marked so that members of the community recognize when they are passing into and out of different cultures (see Create Clear Boundaries, later in this paper).

The existence and widespread recognition of expectations and norms is a necessary but not sufficient element of a successful community.Even well-socialized members may violate these expectations.A successful group must be able to monitor and enforce the rules of their community.(see Encourage Members to Enforce Their Own Rules, later in this paper)

Design Recommendations

In this section, we offer a specific set of recommendations to meet the challenges enumerated above.In particular, we will discuss the following design principles:

·         Promote ongoing interaction.

·         Encourage the creation of reputations.

·         Create clear boundaries.

·         Allow members to govern their own behavior.

·         Encourage members to enforce their own rules.

·         Create an internal economy.

Promote Ongoing Interaction

Strangers have every reason to exploit one another.It is only when one is likely to meet a person again that it becomes sensible to act cooperatively.People must cross each others paths regularly for community to emerge.The community should be designed to encourage initial interactions between members, promote recurrent interactions, and encourage group activities.

Mutual cooperation can be stable if the future is sufficiently important relative to the present. - Robert Axelrod, 1984, p. 126.

Tabi no haji wa kakisute

-Japanese Proverb: You can do whatever you want once youre out of your hometown.

Social contactinteracting with other members of the systemis the basic resource of community.But transitory contacts are not enough.To generate a robust and cooperative community, individuals must come into contact with one another a number of times and expect to see one another and be seen regularly over time.Many valuable resources can only be produced collectively.The system can cultivate cooperation by incorporating activities and functions that require cooperative activity into the design of the world.

 First steps - Encourage initial interactions

For any community to emerge, the first step is to ensure that people come to the system and stay long enough for social connections to form.While this may in large part be the task of marketing, some design decisions can encourage the process.

Because presence on the system in real time is one of the essential ingredients of social solidarity, we recommend that the pricing model not be based on time.In fact, long periods of time connected to the system might be rewarded.Any disincentives to remaining on the system reduces the availability of members to cultivate connections.

Simple presence online is only the most basic prerequisite.Cultivating bonds of familiarity, obligation, and reciprocity takes more than co-presence.Indeed, this process is multifaceted and not easily formalized.Different people follow different paths to social connection.Therefore, we suggest that the system be designed to allow for and even encourage varying levels of participation.Interaction with other individuals and groups should allow for a gentle scaling of action with low entry costs.This means that lurking must be formally supported.There should be ways of subtly signaling overtures and acceptances as well as varying levels of group membership and affiliation, distance, and opposition.These processes are dealt with in face-to-face communication through body language, tone of voice, and subtle differences in markings, clothing, and insignia. It is the initial steps in group formation that must be most strongly supported.This means that tentative connection must be made easy and comfortable.

Priming the system with active, interesting members is a powerful way to catalyze this process (Goodwin 1994).Members who visibly welcome and announce new members, introduce members to one another, and can offer support and reassurance are essential.These hosts or virtual cruise directors can act as community leaders, helping the group to focus its activities and manage its conflicts.

Crossing paths - Promote recurrent interactions

After entering the system, members should be able to come into casual contact with one another.If the cost of contact is high, members will only interact when they are sure they want to create a relationship.Because many flourishing relationships happen in surprising places, the system must create the conditions that encourage casual contact.The simplest model for this is to create the equivalent of pedestrian spaces that cause members that are in route to other parts of the system to cross one anothers paths.Bonds may not be formed in such public spaces, but the raw materials of such relationships (recognition, familiarity, and some notions of identity) are.

Such spaces for casual contact may require that one of the basic aspects of virtual spaces be intentionally limited.Most virtual spaces allow for instantaneous relocation from one point in the space to any other.Such teleportation is often seen as a way to improve and simplify the use of such systems.We suggest that teleportation be offered only in limited and restricted forms.Because ease of transport may still be desirable, we suggest that differential costs be associated with various forms of transportation that are inversely proportional to the social quality of the journey (see Create an Internal Economy, later in this paper).

Small groups can sustain themselves in ways that large groups cannot.Capitalizing on this small group effect is another way the system can encourage the emergence of community.Small groups can communicate and coordinate ideas and activities far easier than large ones. To capture this effect for the system, we suggest that the space be subdivided into small areas.Each area should provide a homefor each user along with a central common area providing transportation services and information.Homes may be places for storing objects or settings that provide privacy and intimacy, along with a showcase of personal identity.But like interaction in general, homes should have a range of spaces that run the spectrum between private and public.Porches, for example, allow people to be at home while accessible to passersby and neighbors.Similarly, the social physics of the system can make certain activities easier to accomplish (or available at all) when a certain number of people are co-present.Crowded rooms may have capacities that do not exist in sparsely populated rooms.

These architectural choices increase the density of by-the-way information about the world and its citizens.Many of these design considerations rely on the availability and maintenance of at least one rigid framework of spatial connection.If people are to be required to walk along streets so that they come into casual contact with one another, a single unified space must exist.This need not preclude the connection of other multidimensional spaces, but it does mean that users and system designers restrict themselves to some spatial regularity in a primary frame of reference.

Work together - Encourage group activities

After they are brought together, members should be encouraged to work together.There are a number of ways to accomplish this, most simply by creating a range of activities that cannot be accomplished alone.For example, building simple structures may be possible working alone, but more complex buildings and objects may require two or more members to coordinate their construction.Moving large building elements, for example, may require multiple members.

We are therefore recommending that the system be designed so that otherwise solitary activity be restructured as social activity.Further, already social activities, such as games and conversations, should also be supported.Integrating text-based conferencing tools is one way to accomplish this, but may pose special challenges for a graphical system.

Working together may also mean engaging in conflict with one another.Social friction, even when it springs into flame, can be an important element of social cohesion.It seems to be widely recognized that users will engage in conflict with each other with little or no help.However, successfully overcoming a challenge to an entire community is a powerful source of group solidarity (Sherif et al. 1961).Although these crises do have dangerous potential, it may be worthwhile for the system designers to create or cultivate challenges that require whole groups to mobilize.

Encourage the Creation of Reputations

Cooperative relations require that members be identifiable to each other so that reputations might form.Reputations serve as both an incentive for cooperative behavior and a disincentive for selfish behavior.Bodies and identities should be distinctive and there should be a multitude of ways of displaying interests and intentions.

The ability to recognize the other [person] from past interactions, and to remember the relevant features of those interactions, is necessary to sustain cooperation. - Robert Axelrod, 1984, p. 139

Reputations are the foundation of stable relationships

Reputations are the product of ongoing interactions.But ongoing interaction between anonymous members is not sufficient to produce cooperative relations.Unless it is possible for members to develop, display, and recognize their own and others reputations, cooperative relationships cannot survive.A society of strangers is an oxymoron.

But reputations rest upon recognition and identity, and the variability of identity is often one of the desirable features of online systems.A spectrum exists between a system in which identity is fixed and always discoverable and a system in which identity could be continuously reworked and even hidden entirely. A central attraction of a graphical system is the ability to create a range of bodies.It seems likely that members will want to experiment, developing a range of different bodies for different moods and situations.They may also want to engage in completely anonymous interactioncertain interests and activities are too costly if real-world identities are easily associated with online personas.In some cases, members who create interesting and attractive bodies may be asked to share their designs, creating small armies of identical characters.Balancing the conflicting desire for playful identity development and the need for accountability and recognition (in the positive and negative sense) is a difficult challenge.

Flexible identities pose a threat to cooperative relations in the community.Perhaps most damaging would be the ability to perfectly impersonate another member.Absolute anonymity could also be dangerous.The use ofmultiple bodies may be the least threatening, so long as a unitary identity remains discoverable.Bodies should be as distinctive as possible to make identification ofparticular members easier.To resolve these conflicting interests, we also suggest that some form of license plate identifier tag be available no matter what form the member has created for their body.

One suggestion might be that a semi-autonomous zone be created within the larger space that allows some higher level of anonymity or even mimicry.However, this may not be desirable.The WELL, for example, experimented briefly with anonymous conferences.The system as a whole retained its policy of identified postings, but a small group of conferences allowed anyone to change their user identification to anything, including another members identity.Chaos ensued.Members used this special space to denounce and puppet one another.Had the interactions in these groups been contained there, this may not have been damaging.However, members who did not want to participate anonymously observed the activities in these groups and took word of them out to the traditional groups.The result was damage to many peoples reputations and the venting of malice that would otherwise have remained private.

Just as it is important to make bodies as identifiable as possible, it is also important to make members interests and intentions easily displayable.It is important to allow members to recognize in others a shared interest or membership.In face-to-face interaction, there are a range of familiar tickets to begin an interaction.These are forms of passive signaling (for example, buttons, T-shirts, hats, lapel pins, clothing, particular colors, etc.) that offer members a justification to begin an interaction with otherwise unknown others.While the equivalent of buttons has been employed in text-based MOOs, they demand active attentionyou must explicitly request to read them.In a graphical space, passive signals can be displayed without members explicitly requesting them.The move from a text- to a graphics-based environment shifts the balance between expressions that are given (consciously expressed) towards expressions that are given-off (unconsciously emitted;see Goffman 1959).

Create Clear Boundaries

Boundaries are the containers of culture.Groups must be able to draw boundaries around themselves, both to create and maintain a sense of identity and to protect and regulate the resources they collectively produce.Boundaries can include barriers to access as well as methods for banishing individuals.Boundaries must also be visible and obvious to signal to members when they are crossing into different territories.

So long as the boundaries of the resource ... remain uncertain, no one knows what is being managed or for whom. - Elinor Ostrom, 1990, p. 91.

Good fences make good neighbors. - Robert Frost

Allow groups to create boundaries around themselves

If this world does succeed, groups will form to create resources that they could not create individually.These collective resources must be protected in many ways.To the extent that groups produce resources that are publicly available, there is a strong temptation to use the resources without moderation or contribution.Further, each group will have its own subculture that defines its own specific norms and expectations concerning these resources.It is important that these groups be able to tailor their cultures to their particular needs.Similarly, the nature of the boundaries groups create must be flexible as well.Carefully constructed boundaries encourage group interaction.

Any discussion of boundaries requires that we have some understanding of what is being protected.This isnt as simple as it may seem.Groups may wish to bound a space, a discussion, or a collection of objects, whatever they may be.Further, there are many kinds of boundaries.Every community and group has some inherent boundaries that serve as filters, whether they are explicit or implicit.For example, it would seem that anyone can join the WELL, but the difficult user interface, the cost, and its obscurity combine to exclude many potential members.

Boundaries to access can also be explicit.Just as there are private mailing lists, groups should be able to limit their membership.A fundamental example of this is the members home.Additional examples include private clubs and gated neighborhoods.Other groups manage their boundaries by allowing open access but retain strong powers of banishment for transgressions.This can be understood as a kind of group killfile.An open access policy backed by banishment can have higher costs.Kicking someone out of a group is a more difficult task (especially if it requires consensus) than simply excluding them from the start (Kollock and Smith 1995).

Boundaries provide groups with a powerful resource for social control.The power to effectively banish or constrain members activities allows groups to enforce their norms and expectations (see Allow Members to Govern Their Own Behavior and Encourage Members to Enforce Their Own Rules, later in this paper).Boundaries may also operate at a smaller level, all the way down to the individual, who should be able to limit who has access to them by blocking the messages and presence of particular members.

This raises some serious issues.Not all groups should have the right to remain exclusive.It may be necessary to limit the capacity to enforce exclusivity when groups gain significant social power.In addition, there is the real possibility that groups will create norms and expectations that are illegal or distasteful and will require that their boundaries be removed and the groups disbanded.This points to the role for an external authority (that is, the system administrators), an issue we address in Encourage Members to Enforce Their Own Rules, later in this paper.

A powerful model for dealing with boundaries can be drawn from the UNIX file permissions system.A group should be able to allow outsiders to selectively enter their spaces, join their discussions, and take or copy their objects.Or, it should be possible to limit this access so that only a member of a group can open these resources to an outsider.

A carefully constructed system of boundaries will have the effect of creating a community of communities.For this to be sustainable, it is necessary for members to recognize when they are passing from one community into another.Different communities will invariably have different norms, expectations, and potentially even rules of physics.As a result, boundaries should be visible and obvious.

Make boundaries obvious

Contexts are signaled by boundaries.When boundaries are not clearly signaled, members may, like the users of AOL, find themselves in spaces that they do not recognize as different from their own.When AOL added access to Usenet discussion groups, the link was so seamless that users frequently ignored local norms and expectations and applied their own instead.This resulted in conflicts between existing Usenet members and the new AOL arrivals, resulting in new newsgroups such as alt.aol.sucks.Instead, AOL should have made it clear that their users were crossing a border.

Clearly marked and advertised boundaries serve the additional function of directing like-minded members to points of shared interest.Properly operating boundaries create Schelling points, spaces that clearly focus certain kinds of behavior and activity.A good Schelling point allows for the kind of gradual development of relationships that was discussed in Promote Ongoing Interaction, earlier in this paper.For example, the naming conventions in Usenet serve as both a boundary and as a Schelling point, advertising the focus of the group.

Allow Members to Govern Their Own Behavior

Promote self-governance in a federated system.Groups should be able to design rules that govern their own behavior which are well matched to local needs and conditions.External authorities should not unnecessarily interfere with group self-rule.Further, those group members subject to a set of rules should be able to participate in creating, communicating, and changing them.

Support a federated system of governance

To the extent that it is possible, groups that form in the virtual space should regulate themselves.Self-regulation avoids many of the problems faced by a central or external authority.External regulation is costly, may be objectionable to the community members, and ultimately is incapable of widespread social control.But for self-regulation to occur, the members of groups must have the resources and rights to govern themselves.Specifically, groups must design a set of rules that are well matched to their local needs and conditions (Ostrom 1990).This also implies that external authorities should not unnecessarily interfere with group self-rule.The rules that govern a group should be customizable, rather than a procrustean set of rules that are applied mindlessly throughout the world.Further, if the rules are to be well designed and if members of the group are to recognize them as legitimate, it is important that most of the members subject to the rules be allowed to participate in the process of creating, changing, and enforcing them.This not only follows the ideals of democratic legitimacy, but also draws the design of rules from those most intimate with the issues and needs that face them.

Local regulation may also help relieve the problems inherent in large groups.Self-regulating groups face serious challenges when they grow beyond a manageable size.When small, groups are able to leverage the small group effect that maximizes their capacity to form a consensus and regulate themselves.Further, if groups are going to be able to locally define their rules, only a federated system can accommodate the resulting diversity.We recommend, therefore, that the virtual world be composed of a community of relatively autonomous communities.

Problems raised by self-regulation

This solution is not perfect.There are a range of issues that should be anticipated and planned for.Community standards may conflict with one another, leaving open the question of how to reconcile them.If groups retain total sovereignty, the larger needs of the community as a whole may be challenged.What is reasonable and justifiable for an individual group can have tragic consequences for the community as a whole.Further, there may be some forms of behavior that cannot be tolerated even when engaged in by consensual members of a group.Should some behavior always be considered a crime?Should there be lawless zones within the larger space?Can communities decide to limit their members rights to speech, expression, and association?While this may seem innocuous when the limitation is to require members to stay within the frame of a role-playing game, it is less easy to justify if the community demands a demonstration of, for example,Christian piety.

These issues suggest the need for a virtual world-wide constitution of rights and responsibilities that defines the outer limits of autonomy for individuals and groups.Within these limits there can be great variation.These distinctions are made more acceptable when, as we noted in Create Clear Boundaries, the boundaries are clearly marked and explained (for example, marking the transition into a territory that allows virtual theft or violence).

Encourage Members to Enforce Their Own Rules

Self-governance rests on the groups capacity to control its members actions.This process involves a range of activities including socialization, monitoring, sanctioning, and dispute resolution.Each of these activities is best accomplished by the group members themselves.There should be a wide range of sanctions (from trivial to severe) available to groups.Members should also have access to low-cost conflict resolution mechanisms.In addition, there is an important, although limited, role for external authority.

Self-regulation

Technical solutions are not substitutes for social solutions.No matter how cleverly constructed, the tools to shape interaction will be incapable of restraining everything that is seen as offensive, immoral, uncooperative, destructive, or illegal.In all but the last instance, the group itself is best suited to deal with these problems.However, to do so they must have the right tools and the right to use them.

Support the socialization ofnew members

Many members of a community will follow the rules, norms, and expectations set by that group if they are aware of them and they are comprehensible and coherent.This requires that the community have effective means to communicate their local culture.One of the most effective ways of accomplishing this is to provide institutional memory, a store of past activities, debates, and decisions that are open to new members.To achieve this goal, we suggest that the system provide tools to archive and retrieve texts and other documents along with the recording of synchronous interaction.For example, in many MOOs videotapes are available to record all the activity in a particular room.These tapes have been used to great effect.Recordings are available of past community council meetings, lively social debates, and instructional lectures on using the various features of the system.

If this is done well, the costs of active socialization (in which existing members directly inform new members of the local culture) are significantly reduced. Because socialization is often costly, many existing members can be quickly overwhelmed by the task of socializing large numbers of newcomers.The result is sometimes the withdrawal of experienced members and a complete refusal to participate in socialization activities.To avoid this tragic outcome, we also recommend providing incentives to those willing to perform socialization tasks.These incentives may take the form of tokens, status markers, public recognition, discounts, or other benefits.

Let members monitor their own groups

Like socialization, monitoring is best undertaken by members themselves.Self-policing groups are able to take advantage of the fact that members are in close and frequent contact with one another. Monitoring is useful not only to identify transgressions of local rules and normsit also has the effect of generating reputations.Here is another reason why a formal spatial model is useful.When members are frequently co-present, even when only in passing, they are able to create and update information about each others behavior, reliability, interests, and associations.Although these are aspects of individual identity that are often considered private, in practice social groups and individuals benefit from local public knowledge about members.Such knowledge allows members to refer one another to others who may share interests or are able to provide desired services.These social network connections depend upon a stock of general knowledge about a range of other members.While a directory of interests is one alternative to direct introductions, such a system misses the value of a personal contact that can often ease the formation of a new social relationship.

Let groups sanction their own members

Monitoring others activities will inevitably uncover transgressions of the local rules and norms.Punishing transgressions is an import activity for any social group.Without the capacity to sanction, a group can be quickly overrun by members who deplete common resources or refuse to contribute to common goods.Once again, sanctioning is best accomplished by the community members themselves; members have more detailed local knowledge about the context and content of the transgression.For example, a local group member is likely to know if this is the first time that another member has violated a rule or norm.The result is that sanctions can be graduated to fit the particulars of the transgression.There should be a broad menu of sanctions that vary from the minor to the severe so that a blunt instrument does not have to be used to shape behavior.

Let members resolve their own disputes

Regardless of how well designed a groups rules are, disputes will arise over whether a particular action is in fact a violation of the local rules or norms.In addition, rules may be challenged, their legitimacy questioned, or they may conflict with other rules.These disputes are also best left to the community to resolve.If disputes are resolved locally, the costs of resolution are likely to be lower than if they are brought to an external authority.Further, if an external authority is available, it is likely to be quickly swamped by trivial disputes while eroding the groups internal capacity to self-regulate.

But declaring that a group ought to be able to resolve its own disputes fails to recognize just how formidable a task this is.In general, groups evolve a collection of resolution mechanisms that in practice look far less attractive than a formal judiciary.A common model is resolution by exhaustion, in which a dispute is argued and debated at length, being resolved only when the parties to the dispute lose patience or lack the endurance to continue.This process creates a significant amount of noise and can be annoying.However, it is one of the most common methods and it is often effective.A common alternative is resolution by fiat in which a member of high status or formal power (such as a wizard in a MOO, or a group moderator) simply forces a resolution, perhaps after the failure of the prior method.Between these alternatives are a range of formalized but still local dispute resolution mechanisms.For example, MicroMuse has designated certain members as conflict mediators.

The role for external authority

When an external authoritys involvement cannot be avoided, it is important that intervention be carried out as much as possible in and through existing local institutions.For system administrators to simply step into an ongoing dispute and revoke accounts, banish members, or take and return property is asking for trouble.The system administrators disproportionate power should be used judiciously.A well-known example of this comes from Habitat, one of the earliest graphical virtual spaces.In Habitat, a tool intended to be available only to system administrators fell into the hands of a normal member who then used it to wreak havoc.The initial response was to simply delete the object from the possession of the member.The result was an enormous protest and the shredding of the sense of place that had been carefully built-up over time.Upon reflection, the object was returned to the member and the system administrators entered the system as characters in order to negotiate for its return.This restored the sense of social coherence to the space and created a great story to tell.

Create an Internal Economy

Central planning is impossible, and members are more likely to contribute to the world if there is value in the community and if exchange is facilitated.Members will value the community to the extent that they are able to have a consequential effect on the environment and build social ties.Objects within the community will be valued to the extent they are scarce, and this scarcity should at times be designed into the community.Exchange should be facilitated by providing an internal currency and clearly defined principles of ownership.Members behavior should be organized on the basis of incentives whenever possible rather then simply prohibiting particular behaviors.

Create value internally

Objects and activities that are highly valued will inevitably emerge in any community.However, it is useful to consider the sources of value, both to encourage the development of valuable goods and services in the community and to avoid unintentionally creating obstacles to the creation of such value.

Members will value the community to the extent that they are able to fulfill their key desires.There are many sources of value, but people are primarily concerned with efficacy and intimacy.That is, if individuals are to be long-term members and productive in the community, they must be able to have a consequential effect on their environment and build close social ties.

In addition to efficacy and intimacy, individuals are often concerned with both status and aesthetics.There should be positions of marked respect and power in the community for those who emerge as leaders and key contributors to collective goods.It is also important that members be able to build and display objects of beauty.

Objects in the community will be valuable to the extent that they are scarce.Scarcity can be the result of many factors.An object will be scarce if:it requires many people to produce; takes a great deal of time to produce; requires extensive travel to various areas in the world; is composed of rare natural resources; can only be created by an especially talented person; or if the object is created by someone of great reputation.

In some instances, it may be useful to create some forms of scarcity in the community to encourage certain outcomes.As we mentioned above, if building a better house requires that a number of people work together, this will encourage group activities and encourage ongoing reciprocal exchange between community members.It is even possible that some members will come to specialize in certain challenging tasks and so offer valuable services in exchange for some resource (see the next section).As an additional example, if certain useful and rare objects require raw materials from different areas in the world, this would encourage trading relations between different communities within the world.This argues for a variegated environment rather than one in which each community has precisely the same resources as all the others.

Facilitate exchange

The creation of value in the community will be greatly facilitated if there is a system of internal currency.An internal currency serves two important roles.First, it functions as an efficient store of value.Members can convert objects of value into currency rather than needing to maintain a potentially extensive inventory of objects and structures.The second important function of an internal currency is that it facilitates exchange by providing a commonmedium of payment and a way of fixing prices.In contrast, a barter system is a less efficient means of exchanging goods and services.(In the end, both exchange based on currency and the barter system are likely to coexist in the world.The point is that members should have the option of using currency for their transactions.)

The effects of an internal currency on motivating and coordinating behavior were clearly seen in Habitat (Morningstar and Farmer 1991).The simple fact that goods were available for different prices in different areas of Habitat encouraged some members to become specialists in the transportation of goods in order to realize a profit.In this case, the goods were simply redeemed in a vending machine, but there is no reason the activity could not be made social by creating markets and bazaars for the trading of goods.

As the world becomes larger and more complex, it will probably also be necessary to provide a means for members to enter into enforceable contracts with each other.However, the design of the community should not eliminate all sources of risk (indeed, this would be impossible).Although it is important to provide means for securing the terms of some exchanges, the presence of risky transactions is actually very important.Trust emerges in a group only if there is a test of that trust (Kollock 1993).In a situation in which there is no risk, neither exploitation nor high levels of solidarity emerge.One approach would be to allow contracts, but to attach some cost to creating one so that they are used only in some circumstances.

Healthy exchange also requires a clear sense of ownership, that is, property rights.As we discussed in Create Clear Boundaries, there should be ways of marking an object or territory as the property of a particular person or group.

Avoid central planning

Another critical lesson from Habitat is that central planning is impossible.Instead, the best approach is a well-designed set of tools and incentives so that members are both motivated and able to add to the world.

This is not to say there should be no oversight at all. There will be a role for various kinds of architectural review boards in order to insure some minimal consistency in how objects are built and the world is extended.Further, as we have discussed above, there are many circumstances in which reasonable behavior on the part of the individual can aggregate to cause collective disaster.The recommendations we have proposed are designed to encourage as much as possible local control and regulation of these situations, but there are likely to be a limited set of situations in which the system administrators must step in to prohibit or require certain actions (for example, a limit on the rate at which objects might be built if this threatens to deplete scarce bandwidth or other common system resources).This should be done only when absolutely necessary.In general, it is much better to encourage or discourage various behaviors through the use of incentives rather than simply prohibiting an action by writing code that makes the action impossible.As an example, teleporting (moving instantly from one area to another, perhaps very remote area) is made impossible in some MOOs because it is felt that teleporting is disruptive and confusing.Another approach would be to make teleporting possible but expensive, and in a complementary way, make methods of transportation that have beneficial side effects free or inexpensive.Thus, walking or riding a virtual bus, which creates a great deal of by-the-way interaction, might be free, while solo forms of travel would be priced higher.
